Two Worlds, One System

To understand electrons in a crystal, physicists use two different perspectives. The choice of perspective dramatically simplifies the problem.

üìç Real Space: The Intuitive View

Describes particles by their physical positions (\(\mathbf{r}\)). It's like using a street map. While direct, it's incredibly difficult to calculate the behavior of countless interacting particles.

Key Operator: \( H(\mathbf{r}) \)

üåä Momentum Space: The Wave View

Describes particles as waves with a crystal momentum (\(\mathbf{k}\)). By using the crystal's symmetry, complex calculations become manageable, revealing collective properties like band structure.

Key Operator: \( H(\mathbf{k}) \)

The Bridge: A Fourier Transform

The link between these two worlds is a mathematical tool called the Fourier Transform. It deconstructs the real-space description into its fundamental wave components.

Start with the Real-Space Hamiltonian:

\( \hat{H} = \hat{T} + \hat{V} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 + V(\mathbf{r}) \)

\( V(\mathbf{r}) \rightarrow V_{\mathbf{G}} \)

Arrive at the Momentum-Space Hamiltonian:

\( H_{\mathbf{k}, \mathbf{G}'} = \frac{\hbar^2 |\mathbf{k} + \mathbf{G}'|^2}{2m} \delta_{\mathbf{G}', \mathbf{G}} + V_{\mathbf{G} - \mathbf{G}'} \)

A Concrete Example: The Tight-Binding Model

Let's see this in action with a model for a 1D chain of atoms. Electrons have an on-site energy \(\epsilon_0\) and can "hop" between neighboring atoms with strength \(t\).

Real-Space Hamiltonian:

A sum over all atomic sites \(n\).

\( \hat{H} = \sum_n \epsilon_0 c_n^\dagger c_n - t \sum_n (c_n^\dagger c_{n+1} + c_{n+1}^\dagger c_n) \)

Result in Momentum Space:

After the transform, we get a simple, elegant expression for the energy \(E(k)\) for each momentum wave.

\( E(k) = \epsilon_0 - 2t \cos(ka) \)
