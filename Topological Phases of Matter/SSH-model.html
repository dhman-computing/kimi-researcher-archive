Executive Summary

The Su-Schrieffer-Heeger (SSH) model represents a paradigmatic example of a one-dimensional topological insulator, exhibiting rich phenomenology that has become foundational to understanding topological phases of matter. Originally developed to describe polyacetylene's electronic properties [78], [123], this dimerized chain model demonstrates how simple lattice structures can host robust topological phenomena.

Key Findings

  • Topological Classification: The SSH model exhibits two distinct phases characterized by winding numbers ŒΩ = 0 (trivial) and ŒΩ = 1 (non-trivial), separated by a gap-closing transition at |v| = |w|
  • Edge State Physics: Non-trivial phases host robust zero-energy edge states protected by chiral symmetry, with fractional charge e/2 localization
  • Symmetry Protection: Chiral (sublattice) symmetry serves as the primary protector of topological properties, enabling quantized topological invariants
  • Disorder Effects: Symmetry-respecting disorder preserves topological phases, while symmetry-breaking disorder typically destroys them
  • Interaction Consequences: Electron-electron interactions can renormalize topological properties and induce phase transitions

The model's elegance lies in its simplicity: a two-atom unit cell with alternating hopping amplitudes creates the necessary conditions for topological phenomena. This simplicity has made the SSH model a cornerstone for understanding more complex topological systems and a testbed for exploring the interplay between topology, symmetry, and correlation effects.

1. The SSH Model: Foundations

1.1 Hamiltonian and Lattice Structure

2.1 Winding Number (ŒΩ)

3. Edge States and Bulk-Boundary Correspondence

3.1 Existence and Nature of Edge States

The most striking consequence of non-trivial topology in the SSH model is the appearance of robust edge states when the system has open boundaries. In the topologically non-trivial phase (|v| < |w|, ŒΩ=1), two mid-gap states exponentially localized at the chain ends emerge [36], [73].

These zero-energy modes exhibit wavefunctions that decay exponentially into the bulk, with localization length Œæ that diverges as v approaches w. The number of edge state pairs equals the absolute value of the winding number |ŒΩ|, with extended SSH models supporting higher winding numbers hosting multiple edge state pairs [73], [78].

  • Energy: Zero-energy modes protected by chiral symmetry
  • Localization: Exponential decay |œà(j)| ‚àº e-j/Œæ
  • Hybridization: Finite-size effects cause energy splitting in short chains
  • Protection: Robust against symmetry-preserving perturbations

3.2 Fractional Charge and Localization

The topological nature of edge states manifests in fractional charge accumulation. Each edge state contributes a fractional charge of e/2 when half-occupied, directly linked to the quantized Zak phase Œ≥ = œÄ and polarization P = e/2 (mod e) [56], [185].

This fractional charge represents a hallmark of 1D topological insulators and reflects the charge density wave pinned at the boundary, demonstrating how topology can lead to fractionalization phenomena in condensed matter systems.

3.3 Bulk-Boundary Correspondence Principle

The bulk-boundary correspondence establishes a fundamental relationship between bulk topological invariants and boundary phenomena. For the SSH model, the winding number ŒΩ calculated from the bulk Hamiltonian directly predicts the number of edge state pairs in a finite system [36]:

  • ŒΩ = 1: One pair of edge states (one at each end)
  • ŒΩ = 0: No topologically protected edge states

This principle highlights how bulk topological properties dictate boundary behavior, with edge states representing "anomalous" excitations that cannot be continuously deformed into bulk states without closing the energy gap or breaking protecting symmetries. The stability of these edge states is guaranteed as long as the bulk gap remains open and relevant symmetries are preserved.

Inversion symmetry provides an alternative route to understanding Zak phase quantization. In 1D systems with inversion symmetry, the Zak phase is quantized to 0 or œÄ (mod 2œÄ) [185].

This symmetry offers complementary insights to chiral symmetry, with sub-symmetry-protected topological states potentially persisting even when full chiral symmetry is broken, provided inversion symmetry is maintained [112], [218].

5. Effects of Disorder

5.1 Robustness to Symmetry-Respecting Disorder

Topological phases exhibit remarkable robustness against symmetry-preserving disorder. The real-space winding number remains quantized under hopping disorder that maintains chiral symmetry [78], [31].

This robustness extends to dynamical observables like the mean chiral displacement, which can detect topological phases even in disordered systems [30]. The protection mechanism relies on the preservation of the fundamental sublattice structure that underpins chiral symmetry.

5.2 Impact of Diagonal vs. Off-Diagonal Disorder

The SSH model's response to disorder critically depends on whether chiral symmetry is preserved. Diagonal (on-site) disorder typically breaks chiral symmetry, while off-diagonal (hopping) disorder can preserve it [31], [34].

5.3 Topological Anderson Insulator Phase

A remarkable phenomenon occurs when disorder induces topological properties in an initially trivial system, creating a Topological Anderson Insulator (TAI) phase. Random binary disorder can drive a transition from trivial to topological regime [26], [84].

TAI Mechanism

Disorder-induced renormalization of system parameters effectively drives the system across a topological phase transition:

  • Parameter renormalization: Disorder modifies effective hopping ratios
  • Localization length divergence: Signals topological phase transition
  • Edge state emergence: Zero-energy modes appear despite trivial clean limit
  • Detection: Mean chiral displacement serves as robust dynamical observable

The TAI phenomenon demonstrates that disorder can serve as a tool for engineering topological states, challenging the conventional view of disorder as purely detrimental to quantum coherence and phase stability.

This result demonstrates interaction-induced renormalization of quasiparticle charge while preserving topological classification in the gapped regime.

6.2 Renormalization of Topological Invariants

Electron-electron interactions lead to renormalization of topological properties, primarily affecting response function magnitudes rather than discrete topological classification within certain regimes. However, strong interactions can fundamentally alter phase diagrams [145], [147].

Interaction Effects

  • Perturbative regime: Topological classification preserved, response functions renormalized
  • Non-perturbative regime: Phase boundaries shifted, new phases emerge
  • Critical phases: Gapped out by interactions, driving topological transitions
  • Multicritical points: Enhanced sensitivity to interactions

6.3 Persistence and Modification of Edge States

Numerical studies of interacting SSH models reveal that topological edge states can persist even with substantial Hubbard U interactions. CP-AFQMC studies demonstrate robust edge states against interaction, quantified through spin correlations and entanglement entropy [140], [141].

Interactions can modify edge state character, affecting charge localization and creating new many-body edge phenomena. At quarter filling, edge population shows sharp increases with Hubbard U, suggesting interaction-enhanced charge localization [141].

6.4 Interaction-Induced Topological Phase Transitions

Strong electron-electron interactions can drive transitions between topological phases. Extended SSH models with Hubbard interactions exhibit interaction-driven transitions from trivial to topological insulator phases [145].

The SSH4 model reveals a rich phase diagram containing topological insulator, trivial insulator, and charge density wave phases, with continuous transitions between them [151].

Phase Transition Mechanisms

  • Gap opening: Interactions can open gaps in critical phases
  • Band structure modification: Effective hopping parameters renormalized
  • Order parameter competition: Topological order vs. charge/spin order
  • Continuous transitions: Smooth crossover between topological phases

6.5 Numerical Studies (DMRG, AFQMC)
